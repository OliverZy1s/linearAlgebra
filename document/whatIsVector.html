# What exactly a vector is

# Different perspectives of vector

  • The Physics student perspective is that vectors are arrows pointing in space. What defines a given vector is its length, and the direction it's pointing. But as long as those two facts are the same, you can move it all around, and it's still the same vector, no matter in two-dimensional or three-dimensional space.

  • The computer science perspective is that vectors are ordered lists of numbers. In this context, "vector" is pretty much just a fancy word for "list".

  • The mathematician, on the other hand, seeks to generalize both of these views, basically saying that a vector can be anything where there's a sensible notion of adding two vectors and multiplying a vector by a number.

The details of this view are rather abstract, we could ignore it until the last video of this series.

But the reason that we bring it up here is that it hints at the fact that the ideas of vector addition and multiplication by numbers will play an important role throughout linear algebra.

# Specific thought of vector

Before we talk about these operations, we could settle in on a specific thought to have in mind when we say the word "vector".

# Vectors are arrows in space

We recommend firstly thinking the vector as an arrow in x-y plane. The starting point of the array is rooted at the origin and we can use the coordinates of the tip of the array to represent the vector.

TIP

This is a little bit different from the physics student perspective, where vectors can freely sit anywhere they want in space. In linear algebra, it's almost always the case that your vector will be rooted at the origin.

# Vectors are lists of numbers

Once we understand the concept of "arrows in space", we will translate it over to the "list of numbers" point of view, which we can do by considering the coordinates of vectors.

The coordinates of a vector is a pair of numbers that basically give instructions for how to get from the tail of that vector, at the origin, to its tip.

The coordinates of a vector is a pair of numbers. The first number tells you how far to walk along the x-axis, positive numbers indicating rightward motion, negative numbers indicating leftward motion. And the second number tells you how far to walk parallel to the y-axis after that, positive numbers indicating upward motion, and negative numbers indicating downward motion.

Every pair of numbers gives you one and only one vector, and every vector is associated with one and only one pair of numbers.

# 3-D space

We also recommend thinking the vector as an arrow in x-y-z space. In this case each vector is associated with an ordered triplet of numbers: the first tells you how far to move along the x-axis, the second tells you how far to move parallel to the y-axis, and the third one tells you how far to then move parallel to z-axis.

Every triplet of numbers gives you oe unique vector in space, and every vector in space gives you exactly on triplet of numbers.

# Vector addition and multiplication by numbers

After talking about an arrow(geometric perspective) and the coordinates(algebra perspective), it's time to back to vector addition and multiplication by numbers. Luckily, each one is pretty straightforward.

# vector addition

# vector addition in geometry

We have vectors v⃗\vec{v}​v​⃗​​ and w⃗\vec{w}​w​⃗​​，move the origin of w⃗\vec{w}​w​⃗​​ to sits at the tip of v⃗\vec{v}​v​⃗​​.Then, if you draw a new vector from the tail of the first one to where the tip of the second one now sits, that new vector is their sum.

TIP

This definition of addition, by the way, is pretty much the only time in linear algebra.

Now why is this a reasonable thing to do? Why this definition of addition and not some other one?

The way we can think about it is that each vector represents a certain movement, a step with a certain distance and direction in space. The addition of the vector is just the same as if you moved along the sum of those two vectors to start with.

You could think about this as an extension of how we think about adding numbers on a number line, say with 2+5. It is to think of moving 2 steps to the right, followed by another 5 steps to the right. The overall effect is the same as if you just look 7 steps to the right.

# vector addition in algebra

Let's see how vector addition looks numerically. When you take the vector sum using this tip-to-tail method, you can think of a four-step path from the origin to the tip of the second vector, walk 1 to the right, then 2 up, then 3 to the right, then 1 down.

Reorganising these steps so that you first do all of the rightward motion, then do all of the vertical motion, you can read it as saying, first move 1+3 to the right then move 2+(-1) up.

In general, vector addition in this "list of numbers" conception looks like matching up their terms, and adding each one together.

# multiplication by numbers

# multiplication by numbers in geometry

The other fundamental vector operation is multiplication by numbers. This means the vector multiples with an constant number. It's easy yo understand in geometry, just stretching out or squishing down the vector. For example, 2v⃗2\vec{v}2​v​⃗​​ means you stretch out that vector so that it's 2 times as long as when you started.

TIP

When you multiply the vector by a negative number, then the vector gets flipped around.

# multiplication by numbers in algebra

Numerically, stretching out a vector by a factor of a constant number corresponds to multiplying each of its components by that factor. （call the constant number as scalars）。
