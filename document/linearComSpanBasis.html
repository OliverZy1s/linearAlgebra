# Linear Combination, Span and Basis

Vector coordinates are already familiar to you, there's another kind of interesting way to think about these coordinates.

# Idea of Basis Vector

In the xy-coordinate system, there are two very special vectors.

  • The one pointing to the right with length 1, i⃗=(1,0)\vec{i}=(1,0)​i​⃗​​=(1,0) , commonly called "i-hat" or the unit vector in the x-direction.
  • And the one pointing straight up, j⃗=(0,1)\vec{j}=(0,1)​j​⃗​​=(0,1), commonly called "i-hat" or the unit vector in the x-direction.

When you have a pair of numbers that meant to describe a vector, like (3,-2), now we can think of the x-coordinate of our vector as a scalar that scales i-hat, stretching it by a factor of 3. And the y-coordinate as a scalar that scales j-hat,flipping it and stretching it by a factor of 2.

In this sense, the vector that these coordinates describe is the sum of two scaled vectors. The idea of Adding together two scaled vectors is surprisingly important.

# Question❓：What if we chose different basis vector？

For example, take some vector pointing up and to the right, along with some other vector pointing down and to the right in some way.

Take a moment to think about all the different vectors that you can get by choosing two scalars, using each one to scale one of the vectors, then adding together what you get.

Then，another question，which two-dimensional vectors can you reach by altering the choices of scalars？

The answer is that you can reach every possible two-dimensional vector.

# Linear combination

  • Def. Any time that you are scaling two vectors and adding them like this, it's called a linear combination of those two vectors.

# Span

If you let both scalars range freely, and consider every possible vector that you can get, there are two things could happen:

  • For most pairs of vectors, you 'll be able to reach every possible point in the plane.

  • However, in unlucky case, where your two original vectors happen to line up, th tip of the resulting vector is limited to just this single line passing through the origin.

TIP

Technically there's a third possibility too: both your vectors could be zero.

Therefore, we can introduce another terminology：

  • Def. The set of all possible vectors that you can reach is called the span of those two vectors.

So we can restate the process in this lingo, the span of most pairs of 2-D vectors is all vectors of 2-D space. But when they line up, their span is all vectors whose tip sits on a certain line.

# Think vectors as point

This is a good time to talk about how people commonly think about vectors as points. It's really crowed to think about a whole collection of vectors sitting on a line, or a plane.

So it's common to represent each one with just a point in space, the point at that tip of that vector, where that vector with its tail on the origin.

In general, if you are thinking about a vector on its own, think of it as an arrow. And if you are dealing with a collection of vectors, it's convenient to think of them as points.

# Span in three-dimensional space

If you take two vectors in 3-D space that are not pointing in the same direction, what does it mean to take their span?

Their span is the collection of all possible linear combinations of those two vectors, meaning all possible vectors you get by scaling each of the two of them in some way and then adding them together. You can kind of imagine turning two different knobs to change the two scalars defining the linear combination,the tip of resulting vector will trace out some kind of flat sheet, cutting through the origin of three-dimensional space. This flat sheet is the span of the two vectors.

So what happens if we add a third vector and consider the span of all three of those vectors?

A linear combination of three vectors is defined pretty much the same way as it is for two: the span of three vectors is the set of all possible linear combinations.

  • If the third vector happens to be sitting on the span of the first two, then the span doesn't change, the span is still the same flat sheet.

  • If the third vector is not sitting on the span of those first two, it unlocks access to every possible three-dimensional vector.

# Linearly dependent and linearly independent

# linearly dependent

  • 3-D space: the third vector was already sitting on the span of the first two
  • 2-D plane: two vectors happen to line up

For cases shown above, we want some terminology to describe the fact that at least one of these vectors is redundant, not adding anything to our span, so there is definition shown below:

  • Def. When you have multiple vectors and you could remove one without reducing the span, the relevant terminology is to say that they are linearly dependent.

Another way of phrasing that would be to say that one of the vectors can be expressed as a linear combination of the others, since it's already in the span of the others.

# linearly independent

Similarly, the definition of linearly independent are shown below:

  • Def. If each vector really does add another dimension to the span, they are said to be linearly independent.

On the other hand, another way to phrase is that if any vector can not be expressed as a combination of the others, then those vectors are linearly independent.

# The technical definition of a basis of a space

Go back to the initial topic, a basis of a space is much more than i⃗=(1,0)\vec{i}=(1,0)​i​⃗​​=(1,0) and j⃗=(0,1)\vec{j}=(0,1)​j​⃗​​=(0,1), the technical definition of a basis of a space is：

  • Def. a set of linearly independent vectors that span the full space
